Remark
Teaching goal Technology has become a defining arena of global politics amid the intensifying U.S.–China strategic rivalry. From advanced semiconductors to artificial intelligence and quantum computing, technological capabilities now shape not only economic competitiveness but also national power and global order. Taiwan—situated at the heart of the global semiconductor supply chain and emerging AI ecosystems—occupies a pivotal position within this new geopolitical landscape. Students in both STEM and non-STEM disciplines will increasingly confront the political and economic consequences of technological interdependence, encompassing global supply chains, investment flows, data governance, and security standards. This lecture aims to equip students with interdisciplinary analytical skills that bridge technology and international relations. It trains students to map power dynamics across technological stacks, identify chokepoints and strategic dependencies, and evaluate how states and firms deploy technology as tools of influence and coercion. Through case studies and interactive discussions, students will learn to translate complex technical risks into actionable insights for research, policy design, and industry strategy. Ultimately, the course encourages participants to critically assess how innovation, regulation, and geopolitics interact to shape the future of the global technological order. By the end of the course, students can:  Explain major IR theories and apply them to technology domains.  Map global tech stacks and identify geopolitical chokepoints and substitutes.  Evaluate policy instruments (export controls, standards, subsidies, data rules) and predict technical/market impacts.  Produce executive‑ready memos translating technical risk into policy strategy.  Present a sectoral case (chips/AI/drones/space/bio/energy/minerals) with actionable recommendations for Taiwan and partners.
Teaching content 1. Introduction: Realism and Economic Nationalism
 Key ideas: Power, relative gains problems, mercantilism, and economic statecraft
 Why it matters for tech: export controls, subsidies (e.g., CHIPS‑Act), local content rules, investment screening, friend‑shoring, standards as power.

2. Introduction on International Relations II: International Regimes and Constructivism
 Key ideas: regimes as rule‑sets; norms & identities; path dependence.
 Why it matters for tech: standards bodies and responsible‑AI norms.

3. Technology Development: States, Market, and Research Institutions
 Topics: national innovation systems; industrial policy; university-industry relations.
 Taiwan focus: Hsinchu science park, public–private complementarities

4. Trade, Investment, and Interdependence
 Topics: GVCs, FDI screening, sanctions, supply‑chain resilience.
 Concepts: complex interdependence; weaponized interdependence.

5. Global Capitalism and Financial Market
 Topics: Market Access, Venture Capital, Global Finance
 Case: Tech Giants in China within 2000-2010

6. Standards, Rules, and Regulations
 Topics: 3GPP, IEEE, ISO/IEC JTC 1, IETF; Open‑RAN; safety & conformity regimes; export controls and Wassenaar basics; data governance.

7. Who Takes the Lead? Technological Impact on Global Politics
 Technology as global public goods
 Technology as economic interests
 Technology in international institution
 Technology as tool of monopolization
 Technology as tool of weaponization
