Remark
Teaching goal ◼︎ Course Description & Objectives The term "geopolitics" originated in the interwar period, marking a pivotal shift from international to global politics. The contemporary geopolitical landscape is defined by the interplay between global powers, the interconnectedness of regional relations, and the forces of economic globalization. This course is designed to achieve three primary objectives: Conceptual Foundation: To understand the origins, dynamics, and ideological norms of geopolitical theory. Regional Perspective: To cultivate a comprehensive vision of the current regional geopolitical landscape. Analytical Skills: To equip students with the foundational tools necessary to analyze complex geopolitical events. ◼︎ Course Structure The curriculum is organized into four thematic modules: Part I: The Formation, Dynamics, and Systems of Contemporary Geopolitics Introduction to geopolitics as a discipline bridging theory and practice. Examination of the fundamental ideas shaping modern geopolitics. Analysis of the concept and evolution of the "Liberal International Order." Part II: The Great Transformation of Contemporary Geopolitics Investigation into the fracturing of the liberal international order. Long-term catalysts: Cold War legacies and the evolution of modern warfare technology. Recent developments: The rise of "Civilization-States" and the "Axis of Autocrats" (4-week intensive study). Part III: Global Interconnectedness & Regional Orders A comparative study of four pivotal regional orders: Russia, Europe, the Middle East, and the United States. Part IV: The East Asian Regional Order & The Rise of China China Shock 2.0: Analyzing the rise of China as a defining 21st-century event. Hegemonic Competition: The global rivalry between the United States and China. Focus on Taiwan: A dedicated discussion on Taiwan’s strategic geopolitical position. ◼︎ Assessment & Grading Group Analysis Report & Presentation Students will work in groups to conduct an in-depth analysis of a specific political or economic event. Requirements: Source Selection: Select at least five reports or commentaries from reputable foreign media outlets and think tanks. Case Narrative: Provide a concise preliminary description of the chosen event. Discourse Analysis: Analyze the various perspectives and argumentative angles presented in the selected media. Oral Presentation: Present findings in class to demonstrate proficiency in media literacy and critical discourse analysis.

Grading ◼︎ Course Assessment & Grading 1. Individual Term Paper (50%) Requirements: Students must select a chapter from the required readings and write a critical review (1,000–3,000 words). The report must include a reconstruction of the reading's core arguments and a personal critical commentary. Academic Integrity: Plagiarism is strictly prohibited. Any instance of plagiarism will result in a grade of zero for this assignment. Constraint: The selected topic must not overlap with the group presentation topic. Submission Details: Deadline: By June 17, 2026 (TST). Late submissions will not be accepted. Method: Email to yuheuhsiao@gmail.com or hand in a hard copy in person. Confirmation: The submission is considered complete only upon receiving a confirmation email from the instructor. Please ensure your name is clearly marked. 2. Group Presentation (40%) Group Formation: Teams of 6–7 members (maximum 8). Task: Select a specific political/economic event related to a weekly theme. Analyze at least five reports/commentaries from international media or think tanks. Format: Presentation (Part I): Each member presents for 2–3 minutes on their assigned material. Q&A (Part II): A 15-minute session where the instructor and classmates act as interlocutors; the entire group is responsible for responding. Grading: A unified group grade will be assigned based on the collective performance during the presentation and defense. 3. Class Participation (10%) Attendance: There will be four random roll calls throughout the semester. Scoring: Base score is 5 points. Each attended roll call adds 1 point. Attending all four sessions grants the full 10 points.
