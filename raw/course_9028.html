Remark
Teaching goal This is an introductory course in International Political Economy (IPE) designed to cover fundamental themes in the field. Part I: Theoretical Foundations and the Rise of Free Trade The first section provides the essential knowledge required to navigate the IPE landscape. We will begin by introducing the three major perspectives and two primary schools of thought in International Relations, alongside the key scholars associated with each. We will then discuss the origins of global trade, examining the mercantilist policies of modern European states and the specific conditions that led to the historical shift toward free trade. Part II: The Politics of Trade The second section focuses on the "Politics of Trade," covering the world trading system, trade policy, regional economic integration, and multinational corporations. We will review the formation of the global trading regime, explaining how the World Trade Organization (WTO) conducts negotiations and resolves disputes, with a specific focus on the "Most-Favored-Nation" (MFN) principle. Regarding trade policy, we will analyze theories through three lenses: international politics, societal interests, and domestic institutions. We will also examine the trend of regional integration in the global political economy, concluding with an exploration of multinational enterprises and international investment. Part III: The Politics of International Finance and Global Issues The third section addresses the "Politics of Money and Finance," arguably the core of IPE. Over three weeks, we will examine the international monetary system, exchange rate policies, and financial crises. The final portion of the course will focus on economic development, energy issues, and the future prospects of globalization.

Grading Grading Policy 1. Individual Term Paper (50%) Requirements: Students are required to select one course topic and write a commentary report on a specific international political or economic event. The report must reference at least five sources from foreign media outlets or think tank commentaries. Length: The report should be between 1,000 and 3,000 words. Academic Integrity: Plagiarism is strictly prohibited. Any student found plagiarizing will receive a zero (0) for this assignment. Constraint: The topic chosen for the individual report must be different from the one selected for the group presentation. Submission: The deadline is June 17, 2026. Late submissions will not be accepted. Email: Send to yuheuhsiao@gmail.com. Please note that the submission is only considered complete once you receive a confirmation reply from the instructor. Hard Copy: You may also hand in a physical copy directly to the instructor. Note: Please clearly state your name on the report. 2. Group Presentation (40%) Formation: Students should form groups of 6–7 members (maximum 8). Task: Each group will select a course topic and a specific political/economic event for an in-class analytical presentation. Content: The presentation must include at least five (5) references from foreign media or think tanks. It should provide a preliminary overview of the event and analyze the various perspectives and arguments found in the media coverage. 3. Class Participation (10%) Attendance will be checked via four (4) random roll calls throughout the semester. The base score is 5 points. Each successful roll call adds 1 point. If a student is present for all four roll calls, they will receive the full 10 points.
