Remark
Teaching goal The aim of this course is to explore memory-related issues in everyday life by integrating theories and experimental findings from memory research and neuroscience. It seeks to explain the phenomena and challenges of learning and memory in classroom and social contexts. Memory lies at the core of our identity—it shapes what we think, how we act, and how we envision our future. It is not merely about recalling the past; it also involves how we respond to present situations and plan ahead. Centuries of psychological research and decades of cognitive neuroscience have significantly advanced our understanding of the theoretical and neural mechanisms underlying memory. However, many commercial courses on memory enhancement lack solid empirical support, while theoretical courses offered in academic settings often fall short in addressing real-world memory problems. Therefore, this course aims to bridge the gap between laboratory-based theories and real-life situations by investigating how memory formation and retrieval apply to practical problems in daily life. In addition to lectures, the course will guide students in identifying real challenges they face and collaboratively exploring potential solutions, thereby applying theoretical knowledge to practical contexts and enhancing both understanding and application of memory mechanisms.
Teaching content Course Schedule (Tentative; subject to adjustment)

Week 1: Course Orientation and Overview
An introduction to the course goals, key learning objectives, and how everyday memory phenomena will be examined through perspectives from cognitive psychology and neuroscience. The course format, assessment policies (presentations and exams), expectations for participation, and study recommendations will be explained. Everyday examples will be used to establish the basic perspective that “memory can be analyzed scientifically.”

Week 2: Core Concepts of Human Memory
Drawing on cognitive psychology, this week reviews foundational definitions of memory and the information-processing framework. We will introduce the three core processes—encoding, storage, and retrieval—and discuss the distinctions and constraints of working memory, short-term memory, and long-term memory. Simple demonstrations and classic research concepts will provide a foundation for later topics (e.g., learning strategies, false memory).

Week 3: The Brain and Memory
This week introduces key brain regions and systems involved in memory (e.g., the hippocampus and related cortical networks). We will explain memory consolidation and reactivation, and examine how sleep facilitates learning consolidation, reduces forgetting, and improves later retrieval. Connections will be made to daily routines and learning efficiency to develop practical, everyday understanding.

Week 4: Retrieval and Active Recall (I)
We contrast “re-reading/re-watching” with “active recall” in terms of learning effectiveness, and introduce the testing effect and the logic of retrieval practice. We also introduce the basics of spaced learning and discuss why distributed practice is typically superior to massed practice, serving as a foundation for later strategy design.

Week 5: Retrieval and Active Recall (II)
Building on spaced learning, we examine key parameters (spacing interval, repetition, timing of retrieval, and difficulty) and common misconceptions. We discuss how to plan review schedules in real coursework. Students will translate course concepts into a personalized “retrieval + spacing” learning plan (with optional brief self-tracking and reflection).

Week 6: Cognitive Load and Elaborative Processing
We introduce cognitive load and the “desirable difficulties” principle, explaining why appropriately challenging learning promotes long-term retention and transfer. Using chunking, elaboration, mental models, and concept mapping, we demonstrate how to organize fragmented information into knowledge structures that are understandable, retrievable, and applicable.

Week 7: Midterm Presentation (Week 1)
Group presentations (first week). Topics emphasize integration and application of the first-half content, such as designing retrieval/spacing strategies for one’s own learning context, constructing mental models for a subject area using concept maps, or analyzing memory mechanisms through everyday cases.

Week 8: Midterm Presentation (Week 2) + Midterm Exam
The first class period is used for midterm presentations. The second class period is an online midterm exam administered via the university’s ee-class room platform. The exam is time-limited (40 minutes) and consists primarily of multiple-choice and true/false items.

Week 9: Metacognition and Memory Monitoring
We introduce metacognition and memory monitoring, explaining how judgments of learning shape study decisions and time allocation. Common biases such as overconfidence and familiarity-based illusions are discussed, along with practical self-check procedures (e.g., calibrating one’s judgments using retrieval performance).

Week 10: Emotion and Memory
We examine pathways through which emotion influences attention, encoding, and retrieval, and how stress and anxiety at different levels may alter memory. We introduce mindfulness and emotion regulation as potential ways to enhance focus, reduce interference, and improve learning quality, accompanied by brief practice and reflection.

Week 11: Memory Distortion
This week explains the reconstructive nature of memory and mechanisms underlying false memories, including the misinformation effect, source-monitoring errors, suggestion, and social influences. Through cases and research context, students will understand why “high confidence does not equal accuracy,” and discuss implications for daily communication and media contexts.

Week 12: How Digital Technology Shapes Memory
We explore how digital tools change memory strategies and how knowledge is accessed. Topics include the Google effect, cognitive offloading, and the pros/cons of external memory aids. We discuss the difference between remembering information versus remembering where to find it, and how to balance convenience with deep learning.

Week 13: Memory Training and Learning Myths
We introduce memory techniques and training methods (imagery, association, method of loci/memory palace, and organizational strategies), and use expert memory research to explain how high performance depends on strategy and knowledge structure. We also address common learning myths (e.g., re-reading equals mastery; familiarity equals understanding; one method fits all), using empirical evidence to help students develop more testable and adaptable learning strategies.

Week 14: Memory Development and Dementia
We examine how memory changes across the lifespan (from childhood to older adulthood), and distinguish typical aging from pathological decline (including dementia) in terms of behavioral profiles. We discuss observable warning signs in daily life, as well as risk and protective factors (e.g., lifestyle, cognitive engagement, social interaction), emphasizing scientific understanding and reducing stigma.

Week 15: Final Presentation (Week 1)
Students present in groups (first week of final presentations). Topics focus on applying course concepts to everyday life, learning design, technology use, emotion and stress management, analyses of memory distortion, and/or issues related to development and dementia. Emphasis is placed on argument structure, evidence use, and peer feedback.

Week 16: Final Presentation (Week 2) + Final Exam
The first class period completes final presentations. The second class period is an online final exam administered via the university’s ee-class room platform. The exam is time-limited (40 minutes) and consists primarily of multiple-choice and true/false items.
